Understanding Drone Regulations in Nigeria: What You Need to Know

PanoptesDrones Team‚Ä¢Feb 2, 2026‚Ä¢14 min read

As drone usage in Nigeria continues to grow exponentially ‚Äî from wedding photography in Lagos to agricultural mapping in Kaduna ‚Äî understanding the regulatory landscape is crucial for anyone who wants to fly legally and safely. Nigeria's drone regulations, governed by the Nigerian Civil Aviation Authority (NCAA), are among the most structured in West Africa, and non-compliance can result in significant fines, equipment confiscation, or even criminal charges.

This guide breaks down everything you need to know about flying drones in Nigeria, from registration requirements to no-fly zones, commercial permits, and best practices for staying compliant.

The Regulatory Authority: NCAA

The Nigerian Civil Aviation Authority (NCAA) is the federal body responsible for regulating all civil aviation activities in Nigeria, including unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), commonly known as drones. The NCAA published its first comprehensive drone regulation framework ‚Äî the "Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems (RPAS) Regulations" ‚Äî to establish clear rules for both recreational and commercial drone operations.

The RPAS regulations classify drones into categories based on weight and intended use, with different requirements for each category. Understanding which category your drone falls into is the first step toward compliance.

Drone Classification Categories

Category 1: Small Drones (Under 7 kg / 15.4 lbs)

This category covers the vast majority of consumer and prosumer drones, including the DJI Mini series (249g), DJI Air series (~720g), and DJI Mavic 3 Pro (~958g). These drones require registration with the NCAA but have the simplest permit process. Most recreational and commercial operators in Lagos fall into this category.

Category 2: Medium Drones (7 kg ‚Äì 25 kg / 15.4 ‚Äì 55 lbs)

This includes larger commercial drones used for mapping, surveying, and agricultural spraying. These require more rigorous permits and may require the operator to demonstrate specific training and competency. Operators must submit a detailed operations manual and risk assessment.

Category 3: Large Drones (Over 25 kg / 55 lbs)

Heavy-lift drones used for industrial applications such as cargo delivery, infrastructure inspection, and military purposes. These require the most comprehensive permits and may need individual flight approvals for each operation.

Registration Requirements

All drones operated in Nigerian airspace must be registered with the NCAA, regardless of size or intended use. The registration process involves:

  1. Application Submission: Complete the NCAA drone registration form, providing details about the drone (make, model, serial number, weight) and the owner/operator (name, address, identification).
  2. Documentation: Submit proof of ownership (purchase receipt or importation documents), a valid form of identification (national ID, international passport, or driver's licence), and proof of address.
  3. Registration Fee: Pay the applicable registration fee. Fees vary by drone category and are typically paid annually.
  4. Unique Identification Number: Upon approval, the NCAA assigns a unique identification number that must be displayed on the drone. This is your drone's "registration mark" ‚Äî the aerial equivalent of a vehicle number plate.

Commercial Operating Permits (RPAS Permit)

If you intend to use a drone for any commercial purpose ‚Äî including real estate photography, wedding videography, event coverage, construction monitoring, or content creation for paying clients ‚Äî you need a commercial RPAS operating permit from the NCAA. This is separate from the basic registration and requires additional documentation:

  • Operations Manual: A detailed document outlining your standard operating procedures, safety protocols, emergency procedures, and maintenance schedules.
  • Insurance Certificate: Proof of valid third-party liability insurance covering your drone operations. This protects against damage to persons or property caused by your drone.
  • Pilot Competency: Evidence that the pilot(s) operating the drone have adequate training and competency. While Nigeria doesn't yet have a formal drone pilot licence examination like some countries, operators must demonstrate appropriate knowledge and experience.
  • Risk Assessment: A written assessment of the risks associated with your specific operations, including the types of flights you'll conduct, the areas you'll operate in, and your mitigation strategies.

No-Fly Zones in Nigeria

Several areas in Nigeria are designated as restricted airspace where drone operations are prohibited or heavily restricted. In Lagos, the most significant no-fly zones include:

Airport Zones

Murtala Muhammed International Airport (MMIA) has the largest restricted zone in Lagos. The no-fly zone extends several kilometres from the airport boundaries, covering parts of Ikeja, Oshodi, and surrounding areas. Flying within this zone without specific NCAA authorisation is a criminal offence. General Aviation Terminal (GAT) at the domestic wing has its own restrictions that overlap with the international terminal zone.

Military Installations

All military bases, barracks, and installations are strict no-fly zones. In Lagos, this includes the Ikeja Cantonment, Navy bases, and any active military facility. Flying near these installations can result in immediate equipment confiscation and detention.

Government Buildings

State House (Aso Rock), government ministry buildings, and certain federal installations are protected airspace. In Lagos, the Lagos State Governor's Office, the Federal High Court, and similar facilities are off-limits.

Crowd Gathering Restrictions

Flying over large crowds (generally defined as gatherings of more than 1,000 people) requires specific authorisation. This affects event coverage at large venues, concerts, sports events, and political rallies.

General Operating Rules

Even in areas where drone flight is permitted, operators must follow these general rules:

  • Maximum Altitude: Do not exceed 400 feet (approximately 120 metres) above ground level unless specifically authorised by the NCAA.
  • Visual Line of Sight (VLOS): The pilot must maintain visual contact with the drone at all times. Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) operations require special waivers.
  • Daylight Operations: Standard permits cover daylight operations only. Night flights require additional authorisation.
  • Distance from People: Maintain a minimum horizontal distance of 30 metres from uninvolved persons during flight.
  • Right of Way: Drones must always yield right of way to manned aircraft ‚Äî immediately descend or move away if you spot any aircraft.
  • Weather Minimums: Do not fly in conditions that reduce visibility below safe limits or in wind speeds that exceed your drone's rated capabilities.
  • Privacy Considerations: Avoid capturing imagery of individuals in private settings without their consent. This intersects with Nigeria's data protection regulations.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

The NCAA takes drone regulation seriously, and penalties for violations can be severe:

  • Unregistered Drone Operation: Fines and potential confiscation of equipment.
  • Flying in Restricted Zones: Significant fines, equipment confiscation, and potential criminal prosecution, particularly near airports and military installations.
  • Commercial Operations Without Permit: Fines, business closure orders, and blacklisting from future permit applications.
  • Causing Damage or Injury: Civil liability for damages plus potential criminal charges if negligence is established.
